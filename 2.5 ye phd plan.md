# 2.5-Year PhD Research Plan

## Programme Aim

Develop and evaluate methods for determining whether gastrointestinal (GI) vision-language models and agentic multimodal systems are genuinely grounded in the evidence they present.

The central thesis is:

> Accurate or persuasive outputs are not necessarily faithful. Medical VQA explanations and agentic reasoning traces must be tested for causal grounding, clinical plausibility, uncertainty, and their effects on human reliance.

The programme progresses from a reproducible GI visual question answering (VQA) baseline, through multimodal explanation evaluation and agentic evidence-chain auditing, to clinician-facing safety evaluation. It does not make a clinical-deployment claim.

## Core Distinctions

These terms must be kept separate throughout the PhD:

- **Answer performance:** whether the answer is correct and medically relevant.
- **Explanation faithfulness:** whether the explanation reflects evidence that causally affects the model's answer.
- **Clinical plausibility:** whether the explanation aligns with clinically relevant regions or reasoning.
- **Explanation stability:** whether the explanation remains consistent under clinically irrelevant changes.
- **Model calibration:** whether model confidence predicts correctness; a confidence score alone is not calibration.
- **Clinician usefulness and safety:** whether an explanation improves decisions or instead increases over-reliance.

## Programme Research Questions

1. **Performance and shortcuts:** How accurate, calibrated, and visually grounded are GI VQA models across question types and complexity levels, and to what extent do they exploit language or dataset shortcuts?
2. **Explanation quality:** Which visual, textual, and multimodal explanations are faithful, clinically plausible, stable, and cross-modally consistent?
3. **Agentic evidence chains:** Do retrieval, localisation, memory, tool use, and self-validation improve grounding, or do they create additional persuasive but unfaithful intermediate states?
4. **Robustness and improvement:** Which perturbation, counterfactual, calibration, or training interventions reveal and reduce unfaithful reasoning?
5. **Human reliance:** How do different explanation conditions affect clinician error detection, decision quality, confidence, and over-reliance?

## Scope and Sequencing Principles

- Keep the first study narrow: benchmark answer performance and causally audit visual attribution methods.
- Do not infer clinical trust from heatmaps, overlap metrics, or model confidence.
- Reserve textual and combined multimodal explanations for Study 2.
- Reserve empirical claims about tools, retrieval, memory, and self-validation for Studies 3 and 4.
- Treat Studies 3 and 4 as linked work packages that may form one stronger agentic-robustness paper rather than forcing a separate paper from every six-month phase.
- Prepare clinician-study ethics, power analysis, and recruitment during Studies 2 and 3 rather than waiting until the final six months.
- Write methods and results chapters alongside each study; reserve the final three months for thesis synthesis and corrections as well as the clinician-study analysis.
- Use the same conceptual framework—performance, faithfulness, plausibility, stability, calibration, and usefulness—across studies so the thesis remains coherent.

## Phase 1: Months 0–6 — Study 1, GI-VQA Baseline and Visual Faithfulness Audit

### Working Paper Title

**Do GI Visual Question Answering Explanations Reflect the Evidence Used by the Model? A Leakage-Safe Benchmark and Causal Audit**

### Study 1 Research Questions

1. How much does fine-tuning improve GI-VQA performance across question types and complexity levels?
2. Do attention and Grad-CAM-style attribution maps identify image evidence that causally affects generated answers?
3. How are answer correctness, sequence confidence, and visual faithfulness related?

### Model Conditions

- Unadapted `google/paligemma-3b-pt-224` pretraining-checkpoint control, without presenting it as a zero-shot instruction baseline.
- QLoRA/LoRA fine-tuned PaliGemma on the leakage-safe grouped training partition.
- Separately trained constant-image adapter as the genuine question-only/question-prior control.
- Test-time constant-image ablation as a separate diagnostic, not as a substitute for a trained question-only control.
- Shuffled-image control to test whether predictions depend on the paired image.
- Secondary official-split replication, using a separately trained checkpoint, to quantify possible performance inflation from image overlap.
- A second VLM is desirable for generalisability if compute permits, but is not required for the pilot.

### Data Protocol

- Treat the published official train/test split as a secondary comparability analysis because a MediaEval 2025 audit reported extensive source-image overlap between them.
- At the pinned Kvasir-VQA-x1 revision, reproduce and report the observed 3,821 overlapping image IDs—94.16% of official-test image IDs—plus the image-question duplicate audit before fitting a model.
- Build the primary train/development/test partitions from the union of the public official splits, grouped by source `img_id`, with no image shared across partitions.
- Version the exact dataset revision and report the observed official overlap rather than assuming the dataset card's intended separation holds.
- Document provenance from HyperKvasir and Kvasir-Instrument through Kvasir-VQA to Kvasir-VQA-x1, including image and QA licences.
- Use original images in the primary analysis. Any augmented-image robustness analysis is secondary and requires an explicit parent-source mapping; every derived variant must inherit its parent image's partition.
- Audit primary train/development/test source-image overlap and fail loudly if leakage is detected.
- Lock the grouped test partition before model development and open it only for one-time final evaluation.
- Preserve `complexity`, `question_class`, `original`, `img_id`, and image-variant metadata in every prediction.

### Answer Evaluation

- BLEU, ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L, METEOR, and normalized exact match.
- Preserve clinically meaningful numbers, decimal points, units, negation, and laterality during answer normalisation.
- Results stratified by question class and complexity level.
- Primary leakage-safe results separated from secondary official-protocol results; quantify the paired or protocol-level difference without presenting the official score as unseen-image generalisation.
- Medical correctness/relevance on a predefined, stratified review subset if suitable expert review is available.
- Paired comparisons and bootstrap confidence intervals, not point estimates alone.
- A fixed evaluation implementation documented separately from any official challenge scoring implementation.
- Interpret the metric set jointly: no single aggregate score establishes medical quality, and class/complexity imbalance can make overall averages misleading.

### Visual-Faithfulness Evaluation

- Compare answer-token-specific vision attention and Grad-CAM-style attribution.
- Preselect approximately one metadata-balanced QA per held-out source image before inspecting predictions; use the full grouped test only for answer benchmarking.
- Include random-map, random-region, and least-salient controls.
- Run deletion and insertion curves over several region fractions.
- Use at least two deletion treatments, such as grey replacement and blur, to detect masking artefacts.
- Repeat random controls with fixed independent seeds.
- Use the saved deterministic answer as a fixed target and score its mean teacher-forced token log probability under every image intervention.
- Define the primary per-level effect as mean random-deletion fixed-answer log probability minus targeted-deletion fixed-answer log probability; positive values indicate that targeted deletion removed more model support.
- Average the effect across locked deletion levels as the primary AOPC-style endpoint.
- Treat regenerated-answer flips, metric degradation, insertion AUC, least-salient deletion, and grey-mask replication as secondary outcomes.
- Use paired source-image cluster bootstrap intervals; average random repeats within each item first.

### Study 1 Boundaries

- Confidence means a documented model score, such as the length-normalised probability of generated answer tokens; it must not be described as calibrated until tested against correctness.
- Attribution faithfulness is not evidence of clinical plausibility or clinician trust.
- Textual rationales, clinician reliance, agentic tools, retrieval, and memory are outside Study 1.

### Phase 1 Outputs and Exit Criteria

- Reproducible data preparation, training, inference, attribution, perturbation, and evaluation pipeline.
- A passed versioned backend contract showing that the project-owned
  `gi_vqa_paligemma_v1` training template exactly matches the direct PaliGemma
  processor after isolating the known built-in ms-swift 3.7.0 one-token
  boundary defect; raw `swift sft` is not an approved Study 1 training path.
- Frozen predictions and per-example metric files for every model/control condition.
- A documented audit of the official split and a versioned, reproducible leakage-safe split manifest.
- Complete full-test results with category/complexity stratification and uncertainty intervals.
- A defensible failure taxonomy based on systematic sampling rather than selected examples.
- Submission target: a MediaEval/Medico-style working note, MICCAI/XAI workshop, or equivalent pilot venue.

Do not progress the main claims to Study 2 until the grouped-split leakage audit passes, the leakage-safe test protocol is locked, and the perturbation results are reproducible across seeds and at least two perturbation treatments.

## Phase 2: Months 6–12 — Study 2, Faithfulness, Plausibility, and Multimodal Explanations

Build the core VLM explanation paper from the Phase 1 infrastructure.

### Main Comparisons

- Vision attention, attention rollout, Grad-CAM/gradient attribution, and occlusion sensitivity.
- Textual clinical rationales.
- Combined textual and visual explanations.
- Raw versus a small number of hypothesis-driven preprocessing/post-processing conditions, such as colour/contrast normalisation, input resolution, saliency smoothing, and fixed versus adaptive thresholds.

### Evaluation

- Faithfulness: deletion, insertion, and controlled image/text interventions.
- Clinical plausibility: overlap or pointing measures against compatible lesion masks, bounding boxes, or expert-annotated regions.
- Stability: agreement under small clinically irrelevant transformations and question paraphrases.
- Cross-modal consistency: whether claimed findings and locations correspond to highlighted regions.
- Accuracy preservation: whether explanation-oriented processing changes VQA performance.

Keep faithfulness and plausibility as separate outcomes. A clinically plausible-looking explanation may still be causally unfaithful.

### Phase 2 Outputs

- Comparative benchmark of visual, textual, and multimodal explanation methods.
- Clinically annotated evaluation subset or a documented mapping to compatible existing annotations.
- Early protocol and ethics preparation for the later clinician study.
- Submission target: MICCAI, MLHC, a medical-XAI venue, or a suitable general XAI workshop/conference.

## Phase 3: Months 12–18 — Study 3, Agentic Evidence-Chain Auditing

Extend from standalone VQA to an agentic workflow containing selected components such as retrieval, segmentation/localisation, memory, tool use, and self-validation.

### Required Comparators

- Standalone VQA.
- VQA with tools or retrieval but no autonomous loop.
- Agent with tools/retrieval and memory.
- Agent with an explicit evidence-validation mechanism.

Audit intermediate states as well as the final answer:

`GI image + question -> retrieved/tool evidence -> evidence state -> memory update -> validation -> answer + explanation`

Measure whether each component improves answer performance and grounding or merely increases the persuasiveness and length of the trace.

### Phase 3 Outputs

- Machine-readable provenance schema for the evidence chain.
- Component-level faithfulness measures and ablations.
- At least one improvement intervention, such as evidence-consistency validation, grounded multitask training, or abstention based on unresolved evidence conflict.
- Submission target: agentic-AI workshop, MICCAI, or applied machine-learning conference.

## Phase 4: Months 18–24 — Study 4, Counterfactual and Agentic Robustness Evaluation

Stress-test the agent and its intermediate states using controlled corruptions:

- shifted or irrelevant localisation masks;
- incorrect or contradictory retrieved cases;
- corrupted tool outputs;
- stale or conflicting memory;
- misleading textual context;
- visual misinterpretation, knowledge deficiency, and context-misalignment cases.

Compare whether standalone models, agents, and agents with evidence validation detect, ignore, or amplify these failures. Report both robustness and the effectiveness of the improvement intervention introduced in Phase 3.

### Phase 4 Outputs

- Counterfactual and tool-corruption benchmark/protocol.
- Failure-mode taxonomy spanning final answers and intermediate agent states.
- Stronger conference paper or journal short paper.

## Phase 5: Months 24–30 — Study 5, Clinician Reliance and Calibration

Run a blinded, randomised clinician-facing study comparing:

- answer only;
- answer plus visual explanation;
- answer plus textual rationale;
- answer plus combined multimodal explanation;
- optionally, multimodal explanation with an explicit uncertainty or abstention signal.

### Outcomes

- Detection of incorrect model answers.
- Clinician decision accuracy and response time.
- Appropriate reliance and over-reliance.
- Change in clinician confidence.
- Perceived clarity, clinical relevance, usefulness, and misleadingness.
- Inter-rater agreement and subgroup/experience-level analysis where sample size permits.

The principal safety question is whether explanations help clinicians identify model errors, not simply whether clinicians report liking or trusting the explanations.

### Phase 5 Outputs

- Preregistered protocol, justified sample size, ethics approval, and analysis plan.
- Clinician-facing safety evidence connecting model faithfulness to human reliance.
- Target venue type: medical-AI journal, human-computer interaction venue, or thesis-synthesis journal paper.

## Programme-Wide Reproducibility and Statistics

Every study should include:

- fixed seeds and versioned configurations;
- training-seed sensitivity for the primary model where compute permits, with any single-seed pilot explicitly labelled;
- immutable test predictions and per-example outputs;
- dataset/model licences and provenance;
- source-image leakage checks;
- predefined primary and secondary endpoints;
- paired statistical comparisons with confidence intervals;
- negative and positive controls where possible;
- compute and failure logs;
- explicit separation of confirmatory tests from exploratory analysis;
- limitations covering dataset labels, distribution shift, explanation approximations, and lack of clinical-deployment validation.

## Dependencies and Risk Management

- **Compute constraints:** complete PaliGemma conditions first; add a second VLM only after the primary pipeline is stable.
- **Attribution incompatibility:** retain occlusion sensitivity as a model-agnostic fallback and document model-specific target definitions.
- **Masking artefacts:** require consistency across deletion treatments and insertion analysis.
- **Annotation availability:** use existing compatible masks where defensible; otherwise create a smaller stratified expert-annotated subset.
- **Clinician access:** begin ethics and recruitment preparation by Phase 2 and retain a non-clinician technical evaluation as a fallback, without relabelling it as clinical validation.
- **Scope control:** later studies may reuse Phase 1 infrastructure but should introduce a distinct research question and contribution.
