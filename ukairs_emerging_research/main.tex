\begin{abstract}
Medical vision-language models (VLMs) are increasingly able to answer questions about clinical images, yet it remains unclear whether their generated explanations are faithful to the visual evidence used for decision-making. This concern becomes sharper in agentic VLM systems, where memory, tool use, and iterative reasoning can create plausible but weakly grounded evidence chains. This emerging research uses gastrointestinal (GI) endoscopy as a case study to ask how VQA models and future agentic workflows should be audited before they are treated as evidence-grounded clinical decision support. Building on preliminary fine-tuning and explanation extraction for GI VQA, the proposed framework evaluates final answers, intermediate tool outputs, memory updates, and multimodal explanations using visual perturbation, counterfactual evidence, grounding consistency, and clinician-facing calibration tasks. The intended contribution is a reusable evaluation protocol and failure-mode taxonomy for safe, evidence-grounded agentic medical AI.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

Gastrointestinal endoscopy is a visually complex and high-stakes clinical domain. Recent medical VLMs show promise for visual question answering over endoscopic images, but correct answers alone are insufficient for clinical trust. A model may produce an answer and explanation that appear clinically plausible while relying on irrelevant image regions, spurious visual features, or post-hoc language rationales.

Agentic VLM systems may offer a more realistic model of clinical reasoning because they can use tools, maintain memory, and iteratively gather evidence before reaching a decision. However, these same capabilities introduce new safety questions. If an agent updates its memory using unreliable evidence, chooses inappropriate tools, or preserves a misleading intermediate conclusion, the final answer may become more persuasive without becoming more faithful.

\section{Related Work and Gap}

GI endoscopy VQA has recently been formalised through datasets and shared tasks such as Kvasir-VQA-x1 and Medico 2025, which provide image-question-answer pairs for evaluating medical reasoning over gastrointestinal imagery~\cite{gautam2025kvasirvqa,gautam2025medico}. In parallel, medical LVLM benchmarks such as MedHEval show that hallucinations remain a central risk for medical vision-language systems~\cite{chang2025medheval}. Recent agentic endoscopy systems, such as EndoCogniAgent, extend VLM-style reasoning with memory, adaptive tool selection, and iterative vision-to-decision workflows~\cite{tang2025endocogniagent}. Emerging challenge-style evaluations of clinical agents motivate hidden stress tests in which structured reasoning traces are judged against incomplete, longitudinal, and sometimes conflicting multimodal evidence, with unsupported or hallucinated claims penalised even when the final prediction is plausible. Existing evaluations largely emphasise task accuracy, benchmark performance, or hallucination rates. Less is known about whether intermediate agent states, tool outputs, explanations, and memory updates remain faithful to the underlying visual evidence. This creates a safety-critical gap for clinical AI: an agent can produce coherent reasoning while accumulating ungrounded evidence.

\section{Research Questions}

\textbf{RQ1:} Do agentic VLM systems for GI endoscopy base their answers on faithful visual and semantic evidence?

\textbf{RQ2:} How do intermediate agent states, including tool outputs and memory updates, affect final answer faithfulness?

\textbf{RQ3:} Can counterfactual and perturbation-based tests identify clinically important failure modes that are hidden by answer accuracy alone?

\section{Proposed Framework}

The proposed workflow compares a standalone VLM baseline with an agentic VLM pipeline for GI endoscopy. The standalone baseline receives an image or video frame and a clinical question, then produces an answer and optional explanation. The agentic pipeline additionally supports planned evidence acquisition steps such as lesion localisation, segmentation, retrieval of similar cases, explanation generation, and validation before producing the final answer.

The key object of analysis is not only the final answer, but the full evidence chain. Each intermediate step can be audited for whether it is visually grounded, semantically consistent with the question, and causally relevant to the final decision. This is particularly important because self-consistency does not guarantee faithfulness: memory, reflection, and tool use can preserve or amplify an initially ungrounded observation. Inspired by structured clinical-agent challenges, the planned audit will also test whether agents acknowledge missing evidence, resolve conflicts between tool outputs, and avoid importing unsupported findings into the reasoning trace.

\section{Evaluation Plan}

\textbf{Visual faithfulness.} Salient or tool-identified regions will be removed, blurred, or masked before re-running inference. Faithful explanations should degrade more rapidly under targeted deletion than under random or background deletion.

\textbf{Counterfactual evidence.} Pathology regions will be removed or modified where annotations permit. The agent's explanation, memory state, and answer should change appropriately when clinically relevant evidence is altered.

\textbf{Tool-output corruption.} Intermediate tool outputs will be perturbed, for example by shifting a segmentation mask or replacing a retrieved case. This tests whether the agent validates evidence or blindly incorporates it into its reasoning state.

\textbf{Grounding consistency.} Medical terms in explanations, such as polyp, ulcer, lesion, or bleeding, will be linked to image regions and compared against available annotations.

\textbf{Human calibration.} A later clinician-facing study will test whether visual and multimodal explanations help users detect incorrect model outputs, or whether plausible explanations increase over-reliance.

\section{Expected Contributions}

This project aims to contribute: (1) an evaluation framework for auditing evidence faithfulness in agentic medical VLM workflows; (2) a taxonomy of failure modes involving ungrounded tool use, misleading memory updates, and post-hoc rationales; and (3) an early benchmark protocol for comparing standalone VLMs with agentic VLM systems in GI endoscopy.

\section{Relevance to UKAIRS}

This work aligns with UKAIRS by connecting clinical decision support, agentic AI, multimodal health data, and responsible human-AI collaboration. It addresses a timely question for the UK AI research community: how should emerging agentic systems be evaluated before they are trusted in high-stakes clinical settings?

\section{Current Status}

Preliminary work has begun with a PaliGemma-based GI VQA pipeline. A notebook fine-tunes \texttt{google/paligemma-3b-pt-224} on Kvasir-VQA-x1 using QLoRA/LoRA, 4-bit quantisation, a frozen vision encoder, and sampled validation for resource-constrained experimentation. Initial inference checks use a LoRA adapter for GI question answering, while an explanation wrapper extracts attention maps, Grad-CAM-style saliency, token-level confidence scores, and medical terminology from generated answers. These components are preliminary infrastructure for the proposed audit; they do not yet constitute a completed clinical validation. The next stage is to convert this baseline into an agentic evidence-validation workflow and evaluate whether each reasoning step remains grounded in clinically relevant visual evidence.
