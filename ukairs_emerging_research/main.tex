\begin{abstract}
Medical vision-language models (VLMs) are able to answer questions about clinical images, yet it remains unclear whether their generated explanations are faithful to the visual evidence used for decision-making. This concern becomes more important in agentic VLM systems, where memory, tool use, and iterative reasoning can create plausible but weakly grounded evidence chains. This research uses gastrointestinal (GI) endoscopy as a case study to ask how VQA models and potential agentic workflows should be audited before they are treated as evidence-grounded clinical decision support. Building on preliminary fine-tuning and explanation extraction for GI VQA, the proposed framework evaluates final answers, intermediate tool outputs, memory updates, and multimodal explanations using visual perturbation, counterfactual evidence, grounding consistency, and clinician-facing calibration tasks. The intended contribution is a reusable evaluation protocol and framework for characterising grounding and reasoning risks in safe, evidence-grounded agentic medical AI.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

Gastrointestinal (GI) endoscopy is a visually demanding and safety-critical clinical setting in which diagnostic decisions depend on identifying and interpreting subtle mucosal abnormalities under substantial variation in anatomy, viewpoint, illumination, image quality, bowel preparation, and procedural artefacts~\cite{beg2017quality}.This project aims to contribute to an evaluation framework for auditing evidence faithfulness in agentic medical VLM workflows, as well as a framework for characterising grounding and reasoning risks involving ungrounded tool use, misleading memory updates, and post-hoc rationales.


\section{Related Work and Gap}

GI endoscopy VQA has recently been formalised through datasets and shared tasks such as Kvasir-VQA, and Medico 2025, which provide image-question-answer pairs for evaluating medical reasoning over gastrointestinal imagery~\cite{gautam2024kvasirvqa,gautam2025kvasirvqa,gautam2025medico}. Early experimental results suggest that domain adaptation and parameter-efficient fine-tuning can improve performance on such tasks~\cite{peter2026parameterefficient}. In parallel, medical VLM benchmarks such as MedHEval show that hallucinations remain a central risk for medical VLM systems~\cite{chang2025medheval}.

Recent agentic medical-imaging systems, including EndoCogniAgent and other iterative VLM frameworks, extend single-pass reasoning with memory, tool use, retrieval, routing, reflection, and repair~\cite{tang2025endocogniagent,alim2026gaze,sayeedi2026route}. However, agency introduces new failure surfaces because an agent may store unsupported observations, retrieve them as evidence, select an inappropriate tool, misinterpret a tool output, or preserve a misleading intermediate hypothesis~\cite{dash2026untrusted,zhang2026memmorph}.

Emerging challenge-style evaluations of clinical agents motivate hidden stress tests in which structured reasoning traces are judged against incomplete, longitudinal, and sometimes conflicting multimodal evidence, with unsupported or hallucinated claims penalised even when the final prediction is plausible. Existing evaluations still largely emphasise task accuracy, benchmark performance, or hallucination rates. Less is known about whether intermediate agent states, tool outputs, explanations, and memory updates remain faithful to the underlying visual evidence. This creates a safety-critical gap for clinical AI where an agent can produce coherent reasoning while accumulating ungrounded evidence.

\section{Research Questions}

\textbf{RQ1:} Do agentic VLM systems for GI endoscopy base their answers on faithful visual and semantic evidence?

\textbf{RQ2:} How do intermediate agent states, including tool outputs and memory updates, affect final answer faithfulness?

\textbf{RQ3:} Can counterfactual and perturbation-based tests identify clinically important grounding and reasoning risks that are hidden by answer accuracy alone?

\section{Proposed Framework}

The proposed workflow compares a standalone VLM baseline with an agentic VLM pipeline for GI endoscopy. The standalone baseline receives an image or video frame and a clinical question, then produces an answer and optional explanation. The agentic pipeline additionally supports planned evidence acquisition steps such as lesion localisation, segmentation, retrieval of similar cases, explanation generation, and validation before producing the final answer.

The key object of analysis is not only the final answer, but the full evidence chain. Each intermediate step can be audited for whether it is visually grounded, semantically consistent with the question, and causally relevant to the final decision. This is particularly important because self-consistency does not guarantee faithfulness: memory, reflection, and tool use can preserve or amplify an initially ungrounded observation. Inspired by structured clinical-agent challenges, the planned audit will also test whether agents acknowledge missing evidence, resolve conflicts between tool outputs, and avoid importing unsupported findings into the reasoning trace.

\section{Evaluation Plan}

The evaluation will examine whether the agent's predictions and explanations are grounded in clinically relevant visual evidence, responsive to counterfactual changes, and robust to errors introduced during multi-step reasoning. Visual faithfulness will first be assessed using perturbation-based deletion tests. Regions identified as salient by the model or selected by external tools will be removed, blurred, or masked before inference is repeated. Changes in prediction confidence and task performance will be compared with those produced by matched perturbations of randomly selected or background regions. Explanations will be considered more faithful when removing the regions they identify as relevant causes a greater deterioration in model performance than removing visually irrelevant regions.

Counterfactual sensitivity will be evaluated by removing, replacing, or modifying annotated pathological regions where the available data permit such interventions. The resulting changes in the agent's prediction, generated explanation, intermediate reasoning state, and memory will then be examined. A well-grounded system should revise its outputs in a manner consistent with the altered clinical evidence rather than retaining conclusions derived from the original image.

Robustness to intermediate errors will be assessed by deliberately perturbing outputs generated by external tools. These interventions may include spatially shifting a segmentation mask, altering a region proposal, or replacing a retrieved reference case with an irrelevant or mismatched example. The analysis will determine whether the agent detects inconsistencies, seeks corroborating evidence, or incorporates corrupted information uncritically into subsequent reasoning and memory.

The spatial consistency of explanations will also be evaluated by linking clinically meaningful terms, such as \emph{polyp}, \emph{ulcer}, \emph{lesion}, and \emph{bleeding}, to corresponding image regions. Predicted regions will be compared with available bounding-box, segmentation, or region-level annotations using appropriate localization metrics. Finally, a clinician-facing study will investigate whether visual and multimodal explanations improve users' ability to identify incorrect model outputs. Performance will be compared across conditions with and without explanations, with particular attention to diagnostic accuracy, error detection, confidence calibration, and inappropriate reliance on plausible but unfaithful explanations.


\section{Current Status}

Preliminary work has begun with a PaliGemma-based GI VQA pipeline. A notebook fine-tunes \texttt{google/paligemma-3b-pt-224} on Kvasir-VQA-x1 using QLoRA/LoRA, 4-bit quantisation, a frozen vision encoder, and sampled validation for resource-constrained experimentation. Initial inference checks use a LoRA adapter for GI question answering, while an explanation wrapper extracts attention maps, Grad-CAM-style saliency, token-level confidence scores, and medical terminology from generated answers. These components are preliminary infrastructure for the proposed audit; they do not yet constitute a completed clinical validation. The next stage is to convert this baseline into an agentic evidence-validation workflow and evaluate whether each reasoning step remains grounded in clinically relevant visual evidence.
